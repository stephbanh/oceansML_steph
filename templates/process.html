The Concept

We wanted to explore any data we could find on our oceans. As we began our search for data, we realized that there is so much out there! We had to hone in on more specific topics to look for data efficiently.

We decided to focus on the human impact on our oceans - or more specifically, eutrophication in coastal regions due to agricultural runoff, and how commercial fishing might be affecting fish populations and trash accumulation in the oceans.

Sourcing and Transforming Data

Commercial fishing vessel data was found via Global Fishing Watch, a site with a purpose to create and publicly share data about human activity at sea. We created a Python script using Pandas to remove unnecessary columns of data, duplicated entries and false positives, then create separate .json files for the types of vessels and each one within the types.

Marine debris data was found through Debristracker, a site whose main mission is to track marine debris which helps to collect and build a large dataset that assists researchers to help tackle the plasticpollution problem. We gathered data from 2012 to 2022 from all organizations that inputted data into the database, which generated more than 1.3 million data points.

Since the marine debris data and fishing data were collected in more concise files than the vessel data, the files were manually merged in pandas and prepared for our needs by removing erroneous columns and dropping rows with incomplete or duplicate data. Lastly we created .json files of the cleaned data to be used for the creation of our visuals.

Visualizations

We leveraged the flexibility of several JavaScript plugins (mainly leaflet and plotly) to modify markers and cluster data on maps and charts. In our larger datasets we wanted to map, we used Turf.js, a library for advanced geospatial analysis to algorithmically smooth the data into cleaner lines.

For the fishing data, we also incorporated the use of Tableau and embedded those visualizations into this website.


Predicting the Unknown

Once we had an understanding of our datasets, we decided to look forward. Using machine learning techniques, we made predictive models to try to understand what our oceans and oceanic life may face in the future.
