Measuring Plastic and Fishing Nets

In these datasets, plastic is overwhelmingly represented in part by how they are counted. Each piece of plastic counts as one regardless of weight or size. This system would count an enormous fishing net as one, just the same as a plastic cigar tip would be counted as one, even though the net is much larger.

However, this system and consequential skew does account for the impact and pervasiveness of plastic and microplastics. These items do not quickly decompose, only break down into smaller pieces that are eaten by animals. This could poison the animals or cause organ failure as they cannot digest it.

It is worth noting, however, that fishing debris like nets and hooks are underrepresented in beach cleanups as they often either sink to the bottom of the ocean, where as much as 70% of all marine debris is estimated to end up, or are pushed out to sea by currents.

Discarded fishing nets in the ocean are extremely harmful as they can entangle and harm multiple animals at once and any animal can become re-entangled. The North Pacific Subtropical Gyre is notorious for having trash islands of accumulated fishing paraphernalia that is very difficult for scientists to trawl for data due to its density. Around these islands also swirl clouds of microplastics and other pieces of trash, torn off their original parts by waves. Ocean trash heavily disrupts fishing and wildlife in the areas it is present.
